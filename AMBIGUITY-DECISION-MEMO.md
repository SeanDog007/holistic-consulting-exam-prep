### Q108 (Index 105) — Green peppers & oxalates → **HIGH-CONFIDENCE CORRECTION**

**Question:** "Which of the following foods is not considered a high oxalate food?"
**Options:** Green peppers | Beets | Eggplant | Onions
**Marked correct:** D (Onions)

**Analysis:**
- Green peppers: ~1-2 mg oxalate per serving — **clearly low oxalate** (Harvard T.H. Chan data, University of Chicago kidney stone diet guide)
- Onions: ~5-7 mg/serving — also low oxalate
- Beets: 75-150 mg/serving — **high oxalate** ✅
- Eggplant: ~9-15 mg/serving — **moderate**, not high

The problem: Both green peppers (A) AND onions (D) are not high-oxalate. A student choosing A is arguably *more* correct since green peppers are the lowest-oxalate option on the list. Eggplant is also debatable.

**Recommendation:** Replace green peppers (A) with a clearly high-oxalate food: **spinach** (~750 mg/serving), **rhubarb** (~540 mg/serving), or **sweet potatoes** (~30-60 mg depending on prep). This makes onions the unambiguous "not high oxalate" answer.

**Confidence: HIGH** — No SME needed. The oxalate data is well-established across multiple reference tables.

### Q569 (Index 564) — Magnesium & sodium retention → **LEAVE AS-IS**

**Question:** "Which of the following is not true regarding magnesium's effect on blood pressure?"
**Options:**
- A: Increases nitric oxide production
- B: Induces vasodilation
- C: Regulates cell communication, allowing the correct amount of calcium into the cell
- D: Reduces sodium retention by the kidneys ✓

**Analysis:**
Ryan's first pass flagged this because some evidence shows Mg supplementation increases natriuresis (sodium excretion). However, re-examining the literature:

- **A, B, C are all well-established Mg mechanisms on BP** (NO production: Shechter et al. 2000; vasodilation: Houston 2011; Ca channel regulation: Altura & Altura 1995)
- **D** claims Mg "reduces sodium retention." The evidence is thin and inconsistent:
  - A few small studies show Mg may modestly increase urinary sodium excretion
  - However, this is NOT a recognized primary mechanism of Mg's BP-lowering effect in standard references (Gropper, Whitney & Rolfes, Houston's magnesium review)
  - The major textbooks and reviews do not list sodium retention reduction as a key Mg-BP mechanism
  - Even if Mg has a small natriuretic effect, calling it "reduces sodium retention" overstates the evidence compared to the other three options which are firmly established

**Verdict:** D is the *least* true statement among the options. In a "which is NOT true" format, this works. The question is testing whether students know the primary mechanisms, and D is the weakest/most questionable claim.

**Recommendation:** Leave as-is. The answer is pedagogically defensible. No SME needed.

**Confidence: MODERATE-HIGH** — The answer isn't factually *wrong* in the absolute sense, but it is the correct "odd one out" in this set.

### Q610 (Index 605) — Low fiber diet for IBS → **SME REQUIRED**

**Question:** "Which of the following recommendations is not appropriate for a client suffering from IBS?"
**Options:** Relaxation therapy | Low fiber diet ✓ | Enteric coated peppermint oil | High fiber diet

**Analysis:**
This one genuinely needs curriculum input:

- **For IBS-C:** Low fiber diet is indeed inappropriate → answer works
- **For IBS-D:** Low fiber (or low-FODMAP, which is fiber-restrictive) IS a standard recommendation → answer is wrong
- **For general/unspecified IBS:** Both low and high fiber diets can be appropriate depending on subtype, symptom presentation, and fiber type (soluble vs insoluble)
- Having both "Low fiber diet" and "High fiber diet" as options with only one being wrong creates a logical trap — in IBS management, the appropriateness of fiber is entirely subtype-dependent

**Why this can't be resolved without SME:**
The question's validity depends on what the HCQ curriculum teaches about IBS dietary management. If the curriculum teaches a general "increase fiber for IBS" approach without subtype distinction, then the answer key is consistent with the curriculum (even if clinically oversimplified). If the curriculum covers IBS subtypes, this question contradicts itself.

**Recommendation:** Betsy/SME must decide:
1. Does the curriculum specify IBS subtype dietary approaches?
2. If yes → rewrite the question to specify IBS-C or add subtype context
3. If no → the question is acceptable within the curriculum's framework, but add a note that "Low fiber" refers to general IBS management, not IBS-D specifically

### Q655 (Index 650) — Coffee/tea for asthma → **SME REQUIRED**

**Question:** "Which of the following is not a well supported recommendation for asthma?"
**Options:** Organic coffee and tea ✓ | Elimination of food allergies | High intake of dietary antioxidants | Using olive oil instead of foods high in animal fats such as butter

**Analysis:**
This is a genuine judgment call between pharmacological evidence and nutritional counseling scope:

**Evidence FOR caffeine/coffee in asthma:**
- Caffeine is a methylxanthine — same drug class as theophylline (classic asthma med)
- Cochrane review (Welsh et al., 2010, updated): caffeine modestly improves lung function for up to 4 hours
- FEV1 improvement of 5-18% after caffeine consumption
- Multiple studies show bronchodilator effect

**Evidence AGAINST listing coffee/tea as a nutritional recommendation for asthma:**
- Caffeine's bronchodilator effect is transient and modest
- No major clinical guidelines list coffee/tea as an asthma management strategy
- Nutritional counselors should not position coffee as a bronchodilator substitute
- "Organic" qualifier is irrelevant to the mechanism — suggesting this is about general health, not bronchodilation
- The other three options (eliminate allergens, antioxidants, olive oil > animal fats) are well-established *nutritional* strategies for asthma, while caffeine is more pharmacological

**Why this can't be resolved without SME:**
The answer key depends on whether the HCQ curriculum frames "well supported recommendation" as:
- (a) Nutritional counseling recommendations a holistic nutritionist would give → coffee/tea is NOT standard advice → answer works
- (b) Any evidence-based intervention → caffeine IS supported by evidence → answer is arguably wrong

**Recommendation:** Betsy/SME decides based on curriculum intent. If the curriculum teaches caffeine's bronchodilator properties, the answer is problematic. If the curriculum focuses on dietary/nutritional strategies (not pharmacological effects of food compounds), the answer is defensible.

### Q64 (Index 61) — B12 & collagen for bone health → **LEAVE AS-IS**

**Question:** "Which of the following is not true about micronutrients that are crucial for supporting bone health?"
**Options:**
- A: Calcium is needed for bone structure and strength
- B: Magnesium helps to activate vitamin D into its active form
- C: Vitamin D promotes calcium absorption
- D: Vitamin B12 stimulates production of collagen ✓

**Analysis:**
Ryan's first pass noted that B12 does play a role in bone metabolism and osteoblast activity. Re-examining:

- **A:** True — calcium is the primary structural mineral in bone (hydroxyapatite). Textbook.
- **B:** True — Mg is a cofactor for 25-hydroxylase and 1α-hydroxylase, the enzymes that convert vitamin D to its active form (1,25-dihydroxyvitamin D). Well-established.
- **C:** True — Vitamin D's primary role is promoting intestinal calcium absorption. Textbook.
- **D:** "Vitamin B12 stimulates production of collagen" — This is **false**.
  - **Vitamin C** is the vitamin that stimulates collagen synthesis (required cofactor for prolyl hydroxylase and lysyl hydroxylase)
  - B12's role in bone health is indirect: it's involved in homocysteine metabolism, and elevated homocysteine is associated with osteoporosis. B12 may support osteoblast activity.
  - But B12 does NOT "stimulate production of collagen" — that's specifically Vitamin C's job
  - The question even has other questions in the bank confirming this (Index 73, 198, 397 all correctly identify Vitamin C as the collagen vitamin)

**Verdict:** The statement in D is factually false. B12 does not stimulate collagen production; Vitamin C does. The fact that B12 has *some* connection to bone health doesn't make D true — the specific claim about collagen is wrong.

**Recommendation:** Leave as-is. The answer is correct. No SME needed.

**Confidence: HIGH** — The collagen-vitamin C relationship is foundational biochemistry. B12's indirect bone effects don't change the fact that this specific claim is false.
